## Email 2: Day 3
**Subject:** The number that predicts everything about your business
**From:** Yanni Papoutsi <yanni@raisereadybook.com>

If I could only look at one metric in your financial model, it would be Net Revenue Retention.

NRR tells me whether your existing customers are spending more over time (good) or quietly leaving (bad). Above 100% means your business grows even if you stop acquiring new customers. Below 100% means you are on a treadmill.

Here is how to calculate it:

(Revenue from existing customers after 12 months) / (Revenue from those same customers at month 0)

If that number is above 110%, your unit economics are working. If it is above 120%, investors will fight over your deal. If it is below 90%, no amount of new customer acquisition fixes the underlying problem.

I wrote a full breakdown on the blog: [Net Revenue Retention: The Number That Predicts Everything](https://www.raisereadybook.com/blog/net-revenue-retention-the-number-that-predicts-everything.html)

## Email 3: Day 7
**Subject:** Why VCs say no to good companies
**From:** Yanni Papoutsi <yanni@raisereadybook.com>

Most founders take VC rejection personally. Here is what actually happens on the other side of the table.

A $150M fund needs to return $450M to its investors. If the fund owns 7% of your company at exit, your company needs to be worth $6.4 billion for that single investment to return the fund. Not $6.4 million. Billion.

That means a VC managing a $150M fund will pass on a company that exits for $500M. Not because the company failed. Because the math does not work for their fund structure.

This changes everything about how you approach fundraising. Stop pitching VCs whose fund math makes your outcome irrelevant. Start matching your company's realistic exit potential to the fund sizes that actually need your outcome.

I broke down the full framework here: [You Have 93 Seconds. Most Founders Waste 80 of Them.](https://www.raisereadybook.com/blog/you-have-93-seconds-most-founders-waste-80-of-them.html)

## Email 4: Day 14
**Subject:** The personal finance side nobody talks about
**From:** Yanni Papoutsi <yanni@raisereadybook.com>

Before I talk about fundraising and exits, there is something more fundamental.

Most startup failures are not caused by bad ideas. They are caused by founders who run out of personal money before the company finds its footing. The founder who has 12 months of personal runway negotiates differently than the one with 2 months. The founder who has zero high-interest debt sleeps differently than the one juggling credit card payments while trying to close a seed round.

I wrote Start Ready because nobody was connecting personal finance to founder readiness. Three numbers tell you if you are financially ready to start a company:

1. Personal runway (months you can survive on savings alone)
2. Monthly flex (your minimum survival cost vs. current spend)
3. Non-retirement net worth (liquid assets minus liabilities, excluding retirement accounts)

If your runway is under 6 months, you are not ready. If your monthly flex is above 80% of your current income, you have no margin for error.

Run your own numbers: [Three Numbers That Tell You If You Are Ready](https://www.raisereadybook.com/blog/three-numbers-that-tell-you-if-you-are-financially-ready-to-start-a-company.html)
