How I Saved a more than 9 figures Exit on a Saturday Morning (While My Girlfriend Waited)

Key Takeaways

During the sale of Syft to Indeed, the acquirer found a variance between forecast and actuals and moved to cut the valuation by 20%. On a Saturday morning, working from the Holborn office, a reframe of client cohort value using growth-adjusted valuation logic reversed the cut and saved the deal. The lesson: your financial model does not stop working when the term sheet arrives. It is your last line of defence in the room where the price gets set.

What Is Client Cohort Value and Why Did It Save an Exit?

Client cohort value is the projected lifetime revenue extractable from a specific group of customers acquired in the same period, adjusted for observed spending growth within that cohort over time. When cohorts show increasing spend per client as they mature, standard LTV calculations understate the true value of the customer base. That understatement can be corrected, and in the right context, it changes a valuation conversation entirely.

Key facts at a glance:

The Setup: Six Months of Due Diligence

Nobody tells you how long a sale process actually takes. Six months sounds manageable until you are living it, knowing that at any point the acquirer can walk away, find something they do not like, or simply decide the price is wrong.

During the sale of Syft to Indeed, I was the finance lead responsible for the model that underpinned the valuation. We had been through seed rounds, a Series A, venture debt, and a convertible note together. The model had survived multiple investor diligence processes. I was, if not confident, at least not afraid of questions.

Then the call came.

The Oh-Sh\*t Moment

The acquirer had been running forensic diligence on the financials for months. Standard process. Then they came back with a finding: actual gross profit performance was below the forecasted figures in the model. Their logic was clean and, honestly, fair: if the company performed X% below forecast, the valuation should be reduced by X%. In a world where you are being valued on a revenue multiple, a performance shortfall translates directly into a lower price. No drama, no bad faith. Just maths.

What they did not frame it as was: the forecast was always aspirational. Any founder or finance professional knows that early-stage forecasts are directional. The rule of thumb across every raise I have worked on is that 80% of forecast is considered strong execution. Missing by a moderate amount is not failure. It is a startup behaving like a startup. But in an M&A process, "it's a startup, these things happen" is not an argument. You need a model.

I was not in the room when this landed. The founders heard it on a call. The now-CEO rang me that Friday evening, and I remember exactly where I was. Walking across a bridge in London. He said: "Hey, how are you doing. We have a problem. Can you come in tomorrow?"

No urgency. Very calm. The kind of calm that means it is serious. I said I could come in that evening.

He said tomorrow was fine.

It was not fine. But I went home, and I spent the evening thinking.

The Saturday Morning Fix

I arrived at the Holborn office the next morning. He told me what had happened and said three words: "Fix it."

Not: "See if there's anything we can do."

Not: "Take a look and let me know."

"Fix it."

What followed was a few hours of going back through the model looking for something defensible. Not something invented. Something real that had been underweighted in the original valuation framework. What I found was this: when you look at client cohorts individually rather than in aggregate, the spending behaviour of mature clients was increasing over time. Clients were not just staying. They were spending more. And the original valuation had not captured that growth trajectory in the way it deserved to be captured.

The parallel I reached for was the PEG ratio: the

Price/Earnings-to-Growth ratio used in public equity analysis. The standard P/E ratio tells you what the market is paying for current earnings. PEG adjusts for growth, because a company growing at 30% per year deserves a higher multiple than one growing at 5%, even if their current earnings look identical.

The same logic applied here. A client base where cohort spending is growing 25% year over year is worth more than a client base at flat spending, even if the current revenue number is the same. The standard LTV calculation was not capturing that growth premium.

So I built the case: our clients were not just loyal, they were accelerating. That changed the effective LTV. Which changed the revenue the acquirer could expect to extract from the existing client base. Which meant the valuation should be higher, not lower, relative to the standard framework.

The numbers worked out to roughly a 25% uplift in expected client value. Not a rescue. A correction.

Key insight: In M&A diligence, the acquirer sets the framing by default. The only way to change the outcome is to show up with a different, defensible framework before the conversation closes. A financial model that has no capacity for reframing is a liability in the negotiating room.

Why This Worked (And When It Would Not)

This reframe worked for three reasons, and it is worth being honest about all of them.

1. The underlying data was real.

The cohort spending growth was an observed pattern in actual transaction data, not an assumption. If I had tried to argue for higher LTV on the basis of forecasts or estimates, it would not have landed. The model has to point to something real.

2. The logic had a credible precedent.

Using growth-adjusted valuation frameworks is standard in public equity analysis. The PEG parallel gave the argument a respectable intellectual foundation. It was not invented for the occasion.

3. The acquirer was motivated to close.

This matters. A motivated acquirer wants to find a way to make the numbers work. An acquirer who is looking for an exit will use any gap to walk. Reading the room correctly is part of the job.

What would not have worked:

Arguing that the forecast was "aspirational" and should not be taken literally. That is true, but saying it concedes the framing. You are essentially agreeing that the variance is a problem and asking for sympathy. That is not a negotiating position.

What This Means for Your Model

Most founders stop thinking about their financial model once the round is closed or the LOI is signed. That is understandable. It is also wrong.

The model that raises your round is the same model that gets interrogated during M&A diligence. It is the document that will be used to argue for or against your valuation when there is real money on the table and someone on the other side is paid specifically to find reasons to pay less.

Building a model with this in mind means a few things:

Cohort data should be tracked from day one. Not just aggregate revenue, but revenue by customer cohort, with enough history to identify spending trends. The pattern I used to save the Syft exit was only available because we had good cohort-level data going back years. Variance should be explainable, not just reported. Every material miss against forecast should have a documented explanation: a market factor, a timing shift, an assumption that turned out to be wrong and why. Unexplained variance in M&A diligence reads as a management problem.

The model should have flex. Not in the sense of being manipulable, but in the sense of being reframeable from multiple angles. Multiple scenarios. Sensitivity analysis pre-built. Alternative valuation frameworks ready to be applied to the same underlying data.

The Part Nobody Writes About

The CEO liked me before this moment. After it, something changed. He has told the story of that Saturday morning in Holborn to other people. Not because of the technical reframe. Because when the problem arrived, there was someone whose job it was to find the answer, who went to find it.

That is what the finance function is supposed to be in a startup. Not the person who reports the numbers. The person who knows what the numbers mean and can defend what they mean when the stakes are highest. The company sold. People who had worked very hard for years became wealthy. That is what the model was for.

It does not take a genius to do the work. It takes someone willing to do the work before they are under pressure.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a cohort value analysis in the context of an exit?

A cohort value analysis groups customers by acquisition period and tracks their revenue contribution over time. In an exit context, it is used to demonstrate the quality and growth trajectory of the customer base, and to support a more accurate LTV calculation than

blended-average methods provide.

How should founders prepare their financial model for M&A diligence?

Maintain clean cohort-level revenue data from early in the company's life. Document the rationale behind every material forecast assumption. Build variance explanations into your monthly reporting so that by the time diligence arrives, every gap between forecast and actuals already has a narrative attached.

What is the PEG ratio and how does it apply to startup valuation?

PEG stands for Price/Earnings-to-Growth. In public equity analysis, it adjusts the P/E multiple for the company's growth rate, making high-growth companies comparable to lower-growth companies on a normalised basis. The same logic can be applied to customer LTV: a cohort with growing spend deserves a higher value than a static cohort, even if current revenue is the same.

What happens when actuals miss forecast during an M&A process?

The acquirer will typically use the variance to argue for a valuation reduction proportional to the miss. The response requires either a legitimate reframe of the valuation framework or a compelling explanation for why the miss was non-recurring and the business trajectory is intact. "Forecasts are directional" is not sufficient on its own.

Is it normal for a startup\'s actuals to miss their fundraising forecast? Yes. Across every raise I have worked on, 80% of base case forecast is considered strong execution. The question is not whether you hit 100% but whether the variance is explainable, the underlying trajectory is healthy, and the business fundamentals that justified the investment are still intact.

Summary

A variance between forecast and actuals is not automatically a crisis. In M&A diligence, it is a negotiating position the acquirer takes by default, and the only way to counter it is with a better analytical framework applied to real data. In the case of the Syft exit, that framework was growth-adjusted cohort value: the observation that a customer base with increasing spend per cohort over time is worth more than a static LTV calculation suggests. The fix worked because the data was real, the logic was credible, and the work was done before the conversation closed. Build your model to survive the questions that come after the LOI, not just the ones that come before it.
